Semester 2 · Key Course

AI and Large Models

Ten sessions. Six assignments. One final project. What I expected, what the course actually delivered, and how I am applying it to real research.

◎
What I Hoped to Learn

I came in expecting to learn how large language models work internally — the architecture behind ChatGPT, how to build AI-powered pipelines, how to automate the research process end-to-end.

I expected the first session to cover transformers, attention mechanisms, or at minimum a neural network. Instead the professor opened with a bearing. A rotating shaft. A vibration sensor. And a question: how do you find a fault frequency buried in noise?

◈
What I Actually Learned

Session 1 was signal processing: Nyquist theorem, FFT, windowing, Butterworth filtering, bearing fault frequencies calculated from shaft geometry. Not AI as a product — AI as applied mathematics on physical signals.

The course built upward from there. ML classification (Sessions 3–5). Then something that shifted how I think about language models: RAG — Retrieval Augmented Generation (Session 8). Not a model that knows everything; a system that knows where to look. Then domain adaptation and transfer learning (Session 10), leading to the final project: CLIMATE_XFER — adapting a model trained on one climate regime to perform accurately in another.

The gap between "AI as product" and "AI as engineered system" is the most important thing this course delivered.

◉
How I Am Applying It

SIF is a signal. TROPOMI measures solar-induced fluorescence as a radiance value embedded in noise from atmosphere, geometry, and sensor artefacts. The signal processing framework from Session 1 — isolate the frequency of interest, filter the noise, validate the residual — maps directly onto how I approach vegetation signal extraction from satellite data.

RAG reframed my literature review process. Instead of reading broadly and hoping to retain, I now structure queries, retrieve targeted sections, and build knowledge incrementally — the way a retrieval system is designed to work.

Domain adaptation is the core methodological challenge in my second manuscript: a SIF recovery model trained on one Southern African ecoregion should not be applied directly to another without transfer. CLIMATE_XFER gave me the vocabulary and framework to handle that rigorously.

AI and the Research

Competency Framework

AI Skills Mastery

Five core capabilities developed through the course. Depth, application, and path forward.

⟨⟩
Signal Processing Fundamentals
Sessions 1–2

What I learned: Nyquist theorem, Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), windowing functions, Butterworth filtering, frequency-domain analysis, bearing fault detection.

Why it matters: Physical systems speak in frequencies. Before machine learning comes understanding the signal in the noise.

Applied to research: TROPOMI SIF is noise + signal. Isolate the fluorescence frequency, filter atmospheric/geometric artefacts, validate residual. Direct transfer from bearing diagnostics to vegetation remote sensing.

Next step: Wavelet analysis for temporal variation in cyclone-driven vegetation stress responses.

◈
Machine Learning Classification
Sessions 3–5

What I learned: Supervised learning frameworks, training/validation/test splits, hyperparameter tuning, cross-validation, confusion matrices, precision/recall/F1 tradeoffs, overfitting detection.

Why it matters: Raw data is useless without structure. Classification forces you to define what you're trying to detect, measure how well you detect it, and improve systematically.

Applied to research: Cyclone impact classification on vegetation: severe damage vs. recovery vs. no change. Model trained on one region must validate on another without leakage.

Next step: Multi-class classification for vegetation response states across ecoregions.

◎
Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
Session 8

What I learned: Knowledge retrieval systems, embedding-based search, relevance ranking, context window optimization, prompt injection risks, generative retrieval patterns.

Why it matters: LLMs hallucinate. RAG gives them a library card. Instead of "does the model know?", ask "can the system find and synthesize?".

Applied to research: Literature review. Instead of broad reading and hoping to retain, structure queries → retrieve targeted sections → build knowledge incrementally. Same architecture, applied to papers.

Next step: RAG over my own research datasets; query TROPOMI archives programmatically for regional SIF trends.

↔
Domain Adaptation & Transfer Learning
Session 10

What I learned: Source/target domain shift, Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD), domain invariant representations, fine-tuning strategies, negative transfer detection, Few-Shot learning.

Why it matters: Real-world models don't work across boundaries without adaptation. A model trained in one climate zone fails in another. Understanding how to bridge that gap is core to applied ML.

Applied to research: SIF recovery models trained in the Zambezi basin should not be applied to the Limpopo basin without explicit transfer. CLIMATE_XFER gave me the framework and vocabulary to handle this rigorously.

Next step: Implement domain adaptation for spatial generalization across Southern African ecoregions in second manuscript.

🌍
Climate Data & Spatial Modelling
Integrated across course

What I learned: Earth observation data workflows, georeferencing, resampling/aggregation strategies, temporal interpolation, climate classification systems, open-source tools (GDAL, rasterio, xarray).

Why it matters: Data quality determines model quality. Understanding aggregation scale, projection systems, temporal alignment is unglamorous but critical.

Applied to research: Every analysis depends on the choices made: TROPOMI pixel size (5.5×3.5 km), temporal resolution (daily, 8-day composite), and aggregation scale (point, 25km grid, ecoregion). These choices change what signal you see.

Next step: Multi-scale analysis: evaluate how cyclone impacts appear at different spatial resolutions.

Reflective Learning

How I Have Changed

The gap between pre-course thinking and post-course practice. What shifted.

Misconception 1: AI = Black Box Magic

Before: Large language models and machine learning felt like alchemy. Input data, output predictions. How did it work? Shrug.

Shift: The course opened with signal processing—mathematics, not magic. Every technique has assumptions, failure modes, and measurable accuracy. The "black box" framing was just intellectual laziness.

Now: I approach AI systems as engineered tools, not oracles. When a model fails, I ask: What assumptions does it make? Where do the data boundaries lie? How would I know if it's wrong?

Misconception 2: RAG is Just Fancy Retrieval

Before: I thought RAG was a solution looking for a problem—search results ranked by similarity scores.

Shift: Session 8 reframed it: RAG is an architecture for *incremental knowledge construction*. The model doesn't pretend to know; the system finds, the model synthesizes. That's different.

Now: I use RAG logic in my literature review: structure queries, retrieve targeted sections, build knowledge iteratively rather than hoping broad reading sticks. The vocabulary changed how I work.

Misconception 3: Transfer Learning is Plug-and-Play

Before: Transfer learning meant "train on ImageNet, fine-tune on my problem." Done.

Shift: Session 10 showed domain shift is real and dangerous. A model trained on one climate regime *will* fail in another. Negative transfer is not rare—it's expected without explicit adaptation.

Now: I test for domain shift explicitly. A SIF model trained in the Zambezi basin is not valid in the Limpopo basin until I prove otherwise. Spatial generalization is methodological work, not a given.

Insight 1: Scale Decisions are Epistemological

Discovery: The TROPOMI aggregation scale (5.5×3.5 km vs. 25 km vs. ecoregion) is not a technical detail. It's an *epistemological choice*. It changes what you can see and what you claim to know.

Why it matters: This became the core novelty of my first manuscript: the first systematic evaluation of spatial aggregation scale in cyclone damage attribution. I didn't know this was missing until I understood the choice.

Going forward: Every analytical choice (temporal resolution, spatial averaging, classification threshold) is a claim about what matters. I'm more explicit about stating it.

Insight 2: "Attack-Resistant" Beats "Right"

Discovery: Perfectionism made my work fragile. A paper optimized for being "right" breaks when criticized. One flaw and it collapses.

Why it matters: The Struggle section identified this as the meta-weakness. The cure is the same as the diagnosis: expose work earlier, let it be critiqued, iterate with feedback rather than internal polish.

Going forward: I'm building attack-resistant work. Smaller claims. Explicit limitations. Data openly shared. Results reproducible. Takes longer to write, more resilient to scrutiny.

Insight 3: Tools Matter Less Than Questions

Discovery: The course taught ML, signal processing, domain adaptation, RAG. But the bigger lesson: none of these are the point. They're just tools to answer research questions better.

Why it matters: I started the MSc wanting to learn "AI techniques." I'm ending it clear that the question (How does cyclone impact vegetation? How does it vary across regions?) comes first. The tools serve the question, not vice versa.

Going forward: Research design drives method choice. Not: "I learned RAG, how do I apply it?" Instead: "This question needs knowledge retrieval. RAG is one answer."

The Growth

I entered the semester as a remote sensing researcher who didn't speak the language of machine learning. I'm leaving it fluent in three: signal processing, domain-aware ML, and knowledge-augmented systems. But the real change is epistemological. I no longer see these as magical black boxes. I see them as tools with assumptions, failure modes, and measurable performance. That's a different kind of confidence — not confidence that my methods are flawless, but confidence that I can evaluate them, critique them, and improve them.

The course that named the problem I already had
